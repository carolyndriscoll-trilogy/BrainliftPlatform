    • Overview of Direct Instruction
      • Direct Instruction (DI) is a highly structured teaching approach developed by Siegfried Engelmann and colleagues. It’s built on the idea that all students can learn when instruction is designed and delivered with precision. DI emphasizes clear communication, careful sequencing of examples, constant checking for understanding, and mastery before moving on. The following sections summarize its key design principles.

      • Placement Testing and Mastery Learning

      • All video content that is supplied to students should be curated based on the results of placement testing. Students will learn more efficiently when the video more closely matches their prior knowledge. Video presentations that assume pre-requisite knowledge that the student does not have are unlikely to be successful.
      • All video content must present information is small increments, with assessments at each interval, to ensure that the student has mastered the material. Test items in mastery tests should precisely match the formats that the student experienced during instruction. A student must score 90% correct on mastery tests to advance to the next level. If a student fails to reach the 90% threshold on a mastery test, they must receive intensive instruction in the topic, followed by a delayed (24 hour) test with new test items. Once the 90% mastery threshold is reached, the student should be advanced.
      • DI in a classroom setting operates on the assumption that 90 percent of the content of each lesson should be review and only 10 percent should be new. For acceleration in online learning, it is assumed that higher ability students can handle more new information, and lower ability students will require more review. Parameters for students of different ability levels could be as follows: Lower ability students keep the 90:10 rule, medium ability students learn with videos containing an 80:20 ratio, and higher ability students undergo a program of 70:30. If ongoing diagnostic assessment shows that a student is not retaining the information with their particular “diet”, a greater proportion of new-to-review information should be assigned.

      • Unambiguous Communication

      • When designing speech for a video, the speech should be as clear as possible. Only the words that need to be said should be said, using words that the student already knows. Speech should exclude vague and ambiguous terms. As an example, the “unclear” text below shows the terms that should be excluded, in bold, to become a clear text:
        • “This mathematics lesson might enable you to understand a little more about some things we usually call number patterns. Maybe before we get to what is likely the main idea of the lesson, you should review a few prerequisite concepts. Actually, the first concept you need to review is positive integers. As you probably know, a positive integer is any whole number greater than zero.”
      • The speech should be coherent so that each idea flows to the next. Speech should exclude information that is irrelevant to the lesson, such as anecdotes and tangents. For example, when talking about lightning formation, the speech should stay on topic rather than interjecting extraneous information about planes getting struck or victims of lightning strikes:
        • “Everyone have a look at this Venn diagram. We use Venn diagrams to compare and contrast information. John Venn must have been a smart man for creating such an elegant diagram. The similarities go in the middle of a Venn diagram, and the differences go on the outsides.”
      • The instructor should be visible when talking, with a plain background to avoid distractions. The instructor should signal and orient student attention towards the part of the material they should be focusing on, such as with their finger or with animations (e.g., highlighter, arrows, circles). Whenever possible, the instructor should populate material from a blank canvas (rather than presenting all the information at once), and pause to explain each item, one at a time. When drawing, the instructor’s hand should be visible, and the words being said should pair with the information that is added.
      • The instructor should speak over complementary visuals and avoid presenting text that is redundant or duplicates what they are saying. The visuals should represent and enhance the speech, rather than simply adding decoration. When presenting text, only small snippets of text should be presented rather than excessive amounts of text. When information needs to be referred to, it should be visible, but when it is not needed, it should be removed from the presentation to allow the learner to focus on the instructor and the new information.
      • The speech of the video should be as human-like as possible, rather than robotic. The tone of voice should be warm and supportive. The face should be expressive with eye contact. The speech should include elements of personalization. For example, referring to the student as “you”, and referring to themselves as “I” and both instructor and student as “we.”

      • Interactive Instruction

      • DI is far more interactive than conventional teaching. At the beginning of learning a new topic, students should be responding at a rate of around 9-12 simple (one word, yes/no) responses per minute. The video instructor should model the material first with minimal statements, then intersperse minimalist questions that require students to respond on screen to simple questions. This promotes attention and information processing.
      • e.g. “Is this fraction more than one?”
      • Student: Yes/no
      • Posing the question first, pausing for 3 seconds, and then prompting the student to answer with, “Get ready”, promotes mental rehearsal and a higher success rate on responses.
      • e.g. “Is this fraction more than one?”
      • Pause 3 seconds.
      • “Get ready.”
      • Student: Yes/no
      • As students demonstrate mastery on simple questions, the questions become more complex, which require elaboration from the student. This will lead to a decrease in the number of responses that are possible to elicit per minute.
      • e.g., “How do you know whether a fraction is more than one?”
      • Pause 3 seconds.
      • Student: “Because there is a larger number on the top than there is on the bottom.”
      • The video instructor, using on-screen prompts for questioning, should proceed in a simple-to-complex questioning sequence. This is one of the 6 “shifts” that are described in more detail in the section, Systematic Fading of Supports.

      • Error Correction

      • The on-screen instructor should confirm and/or praise the student immediately when hearing correct answers. When a student answers a question incorrectly, the on-screen instructor should correct the response immediately by stating the correct answer along with a brief explanation, and immediately repeat the question to the student. Next, the instructor should give several items to “flush” the missed item out of working memory. Finally, the instructor should repeat the missed item in a future part of the lesson to take advantage of spaced practice. For example, let’s say that the student misses the item, 5+3. The error correction procedure goes something like this:
      • Correct: Immediately correct the missed item.“Listen: 5 + 3 = 8”2.
      • Test: Ask the students to repeat the correct answer. “Your turn, what does 5 + 3 equal?”3.
      • Retest: Give several other items to “flush” the missed item out of working memory, then retest the missed item.
        • “What does 5 + 1 equal?” (6!)
        • “What does 5 + 2 equal?” (7!)
        • “What does 5 + 3 equal?” (8!)
      • 4. Delayed test: Retest the item that was missed at various intervals of the day/week.
      • “That’s right, 5 + 3 = 8. Now I’m going to write 5 + 3 = 8 on a sticky note and test you on it throughout the week so we don’t forget!”

      • Brisk Pace of Instruction

      • The delivery of Direct Instruction video content should be fast paced. The video instructor holds the students’ attention by quickly alternating between minimal statements and questions to check for listening and understanding. The pace can be sped up when the student is getting 100% of the answers correct. The pace can be slowed when the student starts to commit errors, such as when the 90 percent threshold that is required to demonstrate mastery is not reached.  As such, too many errors is a sign of pacing that is too fast, and inattentiveness is a sign of pacing that is too slow.

      • Systematic Presentation of Examples

      • Examples should be used to illustrate specific instances of the material.
        • This fraction equals 1 and This fraction does not equal 1.
        • This is a line of symmetry and This is not a line of symmetry.
      • They are either positive, providing a sample of what something is, and negative, providing a sample of what something is not.
      • A video designed with the DI approach should include a series of examples, including non-examples, and not just one example. Providing students just one example is unlikely to help them to appreciate the full range of contexts or situations in which the concept applies, and it will often lead to misinterpretations.
      • It is important when the video first exemplifies a concept to keep the same wording throughout: “This is an arch; this is not an arch” and “This is a line of symmetry; this is not a line of symmetry.” Allowing the wording to be more varied, such as “This is a line of symmetry; This is asymmetrical” can confuse students and inferes with the association between the example and the meaning. In later lessons, the instructor should gradually introduce more varied wording to add richness to the language surrounding the concept. This is the Wording Principle.
      • How examples are selected and sequenced is important. The Set Up Principle, the Difference Principle, and the Sameness Principle should be leveraged to determine selection and sequencing.
      • The video instructor should begin by juxtaposing examples that share the greatest possible number of features. This forces students to attend to just the single feature, removing other possible interpretations. For example, when teaching students how to draw a line of symmetry, the shape should stay constant while only the line is altered. This is the Set Up Principle. In later lessons, the instructor will gradually introduce more varied examples to further expand the range of the concept.
      • To illustrate the boundaries of a concept, the video instructor should juxtapose examples with non-examples that are just ever so slightly different, rather than providing non-examples that are quite a bit different. These “minimally different” juxtapositions are easier to process in mind and avoid leaving interpretation to chance. Continuing with symmetry as an example, if the teacher draws a line down the shape that is just one degree away from being correctly drawn, the students will know more or less the exact point in which a line of symmetry is no longer a line of symmetry. This is the Difference Principle.
      • The previous principles are insufficient for guaranteeing a complete understanding of the concept. As students gain familiarity with the concept through minimally different examples, the instructor should begin to show the range of the concept by juxtaposing examples that differ from one another as much as possible and yet still illustrate the concept. Instead of just using purple shapes, the instructor should vary the colors. Instead of just using polygons, the instructor should show non-polygonal shapes to show that the rule still applies. This sequence fosters generalization and is called the Sameness Principle.
      • For efficiency, higher ability students will require fewer examples, and lower ability students will require more examples. For example:
        • If the student is medium/average ability, present two examples and one non example, and test the student on two unknown instances of the material with yes/no questions in an unpredictable order. Then, as the student will require relatively more examples, present two examples and one non example and test the student on five unknown instances of the material with yes/no questions in an unpredictable order. The unpredictably follows the Testing Principle, which rules out that the student is getting the answer correct by chance. If the student does not reach a 90% correct threshold on the mastery test, present cycles of two examples and one non example and re-test on five unknown instances of the material until the threshold is reached
        • If the student is high ability, present just two examples and one non example, and test the student on five unknown instances of the material with yes/no questions in an unpredictable order. If the student does not reach a 90% correct threshold on the mastery test, present cycles of two examples and one non example, and re-test on five unknown instances of the material until the threshold is reached
        • If the student is low ability, present two examples and one non example, and test the student on two unknown instances of the material with yes/no questions in an unpredictable order. Do this again with two examples and one non example and test the student on two unknown instances of the material with yes/no questions in an unpredictable order. Then, present two examples and one non example and test the student on five unknown instances of the material with yes/no questions in an unpredictable order. If the student does not reach a 90% correct threshold on the mastery test, present cycles of two examples and one non example, and re-test on five unknown instances of the material until the threshold is reached.

      • Systematic Fading of Supports

      • Direct Instruction involves a “gradual release” progression based on the prior knowledge of the learner. When students are properly placed by test, they are matched to material that is next on the “stepladder”. At first, this information is new and unfamiliar to the learner, and thus requires different instructional methods than when the student is more “expert.”
      • Shift 1
        • Start with an emphasis on the teacher’s role as a source of information, shifting to an emphasis on the learner’s role as a source of information.
      • When a student is new to the material, they are novices. At this point, working the material for the student, followed by eliciting student responses (See Interactive Instruction) to check they are listening and understanding, is more fruitful than having students work out the problems themselves. Over time, however, this reverses, with the student doing most of the working out and the teacher mostly asking questions and giving feedback.
      • The “fade” from teacher modeling to student independent practice has several intermediate steps. At first, the teacher models the material in full (always asking questions to check for listening and understanding). Then, the teacher omits a step (often the last step), to have the student complete just one step. This keeps the cognitive load of the task optimized and motivates the student to attend to each of the teacher’s steps. The teacher will continue to omit steps until the student can do the problem by themselves.  An illustration from the book, Just Tell Them by Zach Groshell, is provided above.
      • Shift 2
        • Start with simplified contexts, shifting to complex contexts
      • When students are novices, each new element the instructor presents to them is an item that must be processed in limited working memory. We can call a task “simple” if it contains relatively few of these elements to grapple with. A “complex” task, on the other hand, contains a high number of elements for the novice to process.
      • The instructor must build up to complex assignments by teaching simpler parts of the whole task in isolation. Once students have undergone a bit of modeling and part-task practice of prerequisite skills, the instructor should slowly combine the elements together. The reason this won’t overload students is that they have become so fluent in each of the isolated elements through part-task practice that these no longer constitute multiple interacting elements, but can be retrieved from long-term memory with minimal impact on working memory. By the end of the sequence, the students should be working on the “complex” assignment, but thanks to the foundation that was laid beforehand, the cognitive load it imposes will have been brought down to manageable levels.
      • Shift 3
        • Start with massed practice, shifting to distributed practice
      • When students are first learning something new, it is important that they have multiple opportunities to practice that same task en masse. As student performance indicates that they have mastered an item, the instructor should space out the student’s practice of that item over time.
      • As performance improves, the teacher spaces practice opportunities across time. For example, an item taught in today’s lesson might reappear at the end of the same lesson (immediate review), at the beginning of tomorrow’s lesson (next-day review), two or three days later (short-term review), and again after a week or more (long-term review). The spacing interval widens as accuracy and fluency stabilize. This sequence strengthens retention and prevents the “massed practice fade” effect common in conventional programs.
      • Shift 4
        • Start with immediate feedback, shifting to delayed feedback
      • The instructor should give feedback immediately after each response when students are first being introduced to the material. As this example shows the “set size” is one problem followed by feedback:1 + 5 = 6Feedback: Correct!2 + 5 = 7Feedback: Correct!3 + 5 = 8Feedback: Correct!When a problem is not answered correctly, the Error Correction Procedure mentioned earlier in this document should be employed.
      • As students grow more proficient, the “set size” should increase so that feedback is not given after each problem, but after a series of problems. The students should be working on a large problem set containing problems in random order, with feedback given at the end on total percentage correct. One reason the feedback is not given after each problem is for efficiency: Feedback after each problem slows the lesson down.
      • Shift 5
        • Start with heavy prompting, shifting to unprompted
      • Early in instruction, the system provides strong cues to guide student performance—for example:
      • Prompted: “Point to the numerator. (Pause.) Good. Say ‘numerator.’”
      • Prompted: “The numerator tells how many parts are shaded. Say it.”
      • As students gain accuracy, prompts are gradually removed:
      • Unprompted: “Which number tells how many parts are shaded?” → Student: “Numerator.”
      • Eventually, the student performs without any cues or scaffolds, demonstrating true independence.
      • Shift 6
        • Start with overt responses, shifting to covert responses
      • Early in the novice-expert sequence, the on-screen instructor will want to check that students are listening and understanding with questions that require overt responses. She will need to lean heavily on simple questions that require single word or small chains of words responses. Once the students have demonstrated their new knowledge “overtly”, the instructor will shift to allowing students to work on the material on their own, covertly. This last DI shift may not have implications for individualized online instruction, as all student responses can be collected by the software without a problem.

      • Track Design

      • In conventional instruction, single topics are “covered” over the course of a unit, which is followed by the next unit of study on a different topic. Direct Instruction, by contrast, keeps multiple topics, i.e., “Tracks”, running at the same time. This is to prevent forgetting, but also to help students to see the connections between unrelated topics.
      • A typical lesson could include as many as 4-6 Tracks running simultaneously. For example, a math lesson containing 1st grade content might consist of:
        • Track 1: Counting
        • Track 2: Reading Numbers
        • Track 3: Number Families
        • Track 4: Facts
        • Track 5: Basic Column Addition
      • The selection of these topics is anything but random. Only one of the tracks includes something new, while the others form part of the 90% material for review. Interleaving this new information with review tracks facilitates discrimination; The student must think harder to produce the correct procedure when these problems are mixed in with problems that call for a different procedure. Topics that students are prone to forgetting must reappear in instruction more frequently, such as every day or every other day.
      • As students master the content presented in a Track, the challenge of the Track increases. Perhaps the problems started easy, with single digit numbers between 0-5. As students demonstrated mastery, the problems will begin to include 6-9 and multi-digit numbers. Perhaps the problems appear a certain way at the beginning, such as having a vertical orientation. As students master the material, the Track will start to include horizontal problems, and problems with different surface features that keep the same deep structure. This is the principle of Variation, which promotes flexibility and transfer in problem solving.
      • Put simply, once a topic appears in Direct Instruction, it keeps coming back. Sometimes it comes back just for a day to refresh the student on the topic. Other times, it comes back just before a new unit of study because it is a pre-requisite that students must know inside and out to be successful. Other times, still, old material is presented alongside new material so that students can ensure that students learn to use the right rules with the right problems. An example is mixing subtraction with addition problems to ensure that students don’t blindly apply the operation that they are currently learning but think hard about which operation to apply.

    • Mastery Gates and XP
      • 1. How are we defining a “pattern of weakness” and XP?
      • Each lesson is a mastery gate for a specific fact family.
        • Students must reach ≥ 90% correct to clear the lesson.
        • When they pass, they earn XP, and that XP feedback is immediate after each lesson.
      • On the final mixed mastery test, each fact family appears in 6 items.
        • We define a pattern of weakness as: The student makes 2 or more errors out of 6 in that family.
        • So:
          • 0–1 errors in a family → acceptable
          • 2+ errors in a family → that family is flagged as weak
      • 2. Over how many questions do we trust the pattern?
      • We trust the pattern based on those 6 items per family on the 30-item final test. The blueprint is:
      • Plus 2: 6 items
      • Plus 3: 6 items
      • Plus 4: 6 items
      • Doubles: 6 items
      • Make-a-ten: 6 items
      • Total: 30 items, randomized order
      • All pattern logic is based on performance within those 6 items per family.
      • 3. What happens once a pattern is detected?
      • On the 30-item final mastery test:
      • The student passes only if:
        • Overall accuracy is ≥ 90% (at least 27/30 correct), and
        • No fact family shows a pattern of weakness (i.e., no family with 2+ errors).
      • If they fail (overall score too low and/or any family has 2+ errors):
        • We collect the set of weak families (all families with 2+ errors).
        • For each family in this weak set, the student must:
          • Redo the lesson for that specific family, then
          • Pass a family-specific mastery test:
            • 20 items from that family only
            • At least 18/20 correct (≥ 90%)
            • At most 2 errors allowed (0–2 errors = pass; 3+ errors = fail, repeat)
      • So in short:
      • Pattern of weakness = 2+ errors out of 6 in a fact family on the mixed test.
      • That pattern is judged within the 6-item sample for each family.
      • Once a pattern is detected, students are routed back to that family’s lesson and must pass a targeted 20-item mastery test before we consider that family mastered.

    • Measuring Cognitive Load in Online Coursework: What We Know (and Don’t Know Yet)

    • Cognitive load—the amount of mental effort a learner must expend to process information—can be measured in several ways. In online coursework, two approaches often come up:
    • Pupillometry (measuring pupil dilation), and
    • Self-report (asking learners how difficult or effortful the task felt).
    • Both methods have strengths and limitations, and neither provides a complete picture on its own. This article summarizes the current state of the science, especially as it relates to real instructional programs rather than short lab studies.
    • IR Eye Trackers vs Webcams
    • IR Eye Trackers (Infrared Eye Tracking Systems)
    • These devices use near-infrared light and high-speed cameras to measure small, task-evoked pupil changes. They are considered the research-grade standard for cognitive load measurement because they can detect very subtle dilations (often as small as 0.1–0.3 mm).
    • Webcams
    • Webcams can measure only coarse changes in pupil size. They work well enough to detect extreme states—for example:
    • A learner is significantly overloaded, or
    • A learner is disengaged because the material is too easy.
    • However, webcams cannot reliably detect mid-range cognitive load, especially in the varied lighting conditions typical of online learning.
    • Bottom line:
    • Webcam pupillometry is not precise enough to serve as a standalone cognitive load meter.
    • Self-Report Measures
    • Self-report questions like:
    • “How difficult was this task?”
    • “How much mental effort did you expend?”
    • correlate reasonably well with IR pupil measurements in controlled studies. Because they tap the learner’s subjective experience, they provide information that physiological measures cannot.
    • But there’s a catch:
    • In long-term instructional programs, self-report becomes gameable. Learners quickly learn that giving certain answers affects what they get next in the system. This problem rarely appears in academic studies because most experiments involve one-off tasks rather than months of coursework.
    • Why We Can’t Rely on One Measure Alone
    • Every method has limitations:
    • Webcam pupillometry → detects only extreme states.
    • Self-report → vulnerable to gaming over time.
    • Performance data alone → confounds load with knowledge and skill level.
    • Because cognitive load is multi-dimensional, no single measurement channel is stable enough to guide instructional decisions in real-world courseware.

    • The Emerging Solution: Multimodal Inference

    • The most promising approach combines multiple weak signals, such as:
    • Changes in pupil size (even if coarse)
    • Accuracy and error patterns
    • Response time and hesitation
    • Behavioral traces (pauses, retries, scrolling, replays)
    • Occasional self-report checkpoints
    • Patterns of consistency or mismatch between signals
    • Together, these can produce a more reliable picture of learner overload or underload than any single measure.
    • This multimodal strategy aligns with current trends in learning analytics, HCI, and adaptive tutoring systems.

    • What’s Realistically Implementable Today

    • For typical online coursework:
    • Most feasible now
    • Use webcams as an extreme-state detector:
    • Flag high overload
    • Flag low engagement
    • Adjust pacing or scaffolding accordingly
    • For finer-grained load estimation
    • You’ll likely need:
    • IR eye-tracking hardware, or
    • A multimodal inference model that blends behavioral and physiological data.
    • The latter is the more scalable path for widespread online learning programs.

    • Practical Recommendations

    • Do not rely on webcam pupillometry alone. It’s too imprecise.
    • Do not rely on self-report alone. Gaming issues emerge quickly.
    • Combine multiple signals. Even weak signals become powerful when triangulated.
    • Use self-report sparingly. Checkpoints, not continuous prompts.
    • Treat pupil data as one input among many. Not a direct readout of “cognitive load.”

      • Add a short delay or friction before revealing the final hint or full solution.
      • Randomize numbers, contexts, and wording in practice items to prevent memorization.
      • Lock bottom-out hints when a brand-new concept is introduced.
      • Remove full-solution hints for ~25% of items that require full reasoning.
      • Track hint-usage patterns and flag rapid skipping or immediate jumps to the last hint.
      • Add a self-check prompt before allowing access to the last hint.
      • After showing a bottom-out hint, automatically serve a similar follow-up item for independent practice.
  • Early Math (PK-3) Brain Lift
    • Purpose
      • The purpose of this Brain Lift is to provide research-grounded design guidance for teams developing early math instructional apps. It synthesizes key findings from cognitive science, instructional design, and math education research.
      • By distilling what the strongest studies show about how children actually learn mathematics, the review aims to help teams build (and evaluate) apps that:
        • optimize cognitive load and teach to mastery,
        • accelerate conceptual and procedural development,
        • support generalization and transfer, and
        • avoid common pitfalls that slow learning or produce fragile understanding.
      • There isn’t “Are circle fraction models more effective than bar models for teaching thirds?”-level specificity in the research. Instead, we have to lean on the broader science of learning—cognitive load, worked examples, concreteness, feedback, and spacing— and instructional design procedures, to make our best bets about what the design should be.
      • This review is written to help teams make evidence-informed design decisions, and to ensure that early math apps reflect the instructional mechanisms that consistently produce strong learning outcomes for young children.
    • General Design Requirements
      • There are a number of general design requirements of an Early Math (PK-3) instructional app that have implications for the evaluation and design of apps. These are framed as questions to help with decision-making:
      • Is instruction designed around mastery learning principles?
        • Mastery learning means students progress based on demonstrated competence, not exposure or time-on-task. This matters because early math is highly cumulative: gaps compound quickly, and moving ahead without mastery produces fragile skills, slow progress, and persistent errors.
        • To determine whether instruction reflects mastery learning, evaluators should examine whether the program includes ambitious mastery checks and criteria for advancement, whether it deliberately secures fluency (not just one-off exposure lessons), and whether review is planned to prevent forgetting over time.
        • Mastery Learning Design (2-point rubric)
          • ✅ Highly aligned with mastery learning
            • Mastery assessment and criteria are explicit (clear thresholds for “mastery” and what happens when students don’t meet them).
            • A sequence of teaching proceeds from acquisition, to fluency, to generalization (not just one-off exposure lessons).
            • Adequate spaced repetition and interleaving is built in to prevent forgetting and maintain mastery over time.
          • ❌ Not highly aligned with mastery learning
            • Mastery assessment and criteria are absent; students advance without demonstrated mastery.
            • Instruction does not deliberately move from acquisition to fluency to generalization, or treats these as interchangeable.
            • Review is minimal, irregular, or disconnected from preventing forgetting and maintaining mastery.
      • Is instruction designed around a logical sequence of skills and concepts?
        • A logical sequence means skills and concepts are taught in an order that reflects prerequisite relationships, so students are prepared for each new task. This matters because PK–3 learners rely on secure component skills; when prerequisites are missing, errors look like “carelessness” but are actually predictable results of an ill-sequenced progression.
        • To determine whether instruction is logically sequenced, evaluators should examine whether component skills are explicitly taught before they appear inside compound skills, whether easier forms of the same skill reliably precede harder forms (e.g., recognition before retrieval; oral before written), and whether lesson progression avoids sudden leaps that require untaught knowledge.
        • Logical Sequencing of Skills (2-point rubric)
          • ✅ Highly logical sequence
            • The progression reflects clear prerequisite relationships; component skills are taught before appearing in compound skills.
            • Easier tasks reliably precede harder tasks (e.g., recognition precedes retrieval; oral responses precede written responses; supported examples precede independent work).
            • The sequence minimizes unnecessary leaps that require students to infer untaught knowledge.
          • ❌ Not highly logical sequence
            • Component skills appear inside compound skills before they are taught.
            • Harder task demands appear before easier forms are established (e.g., retrieval before recognition; written before oral; independent work before supported examples).
            • Students are routinely required to rely on inference, guessing, or outside knowledge to proceed.
      • Is instruction designed around observable, measurable behaviors?
        • Programs should define learning in terms of what students can do, say, or write, and fully align teaching, practice, and assessment around those behaviors. This matters because clear behaviors enable clear lessons and clear diagnosis: if the target response is ambiguous, it becomes difficult to know what was taught, what was learned, and why errors occurred.
        • To determine whether instruction is behaviorally measurable, evaluators should examine whether objectives translate into specific student responses, whether practice and assessment match the taught behavior exactly, and whether lessons are sufficiently granular so each lesson adds a small, identifiable component skill to the student’s repertoire.
        • Observable, Measurable Behavior Design (2-point rubric)
          • ✅ Highly observable and measurable
            • What is taught aligns exactly with what is practiced and assessed.
            • Student responses are defined as observable behaviors (what students say/do/write is clear and measurable).
            • Lessons are instructionally granular; one component skill is added to the student’s existing repertoire of skills at a time.
          • ❌ Not highly observable and measurable
            • What is taught does not align cleanly with what is practiced and assessed.
            • The correct student response, or range of correct responses, cannot be identified or measured reliably.
            • Lessons bundle multiple new component skills at once, making learning failure hard to interpret.
      • Does instruction utilize effective and efficient explicit strategies?
        • Explicit strategies are clearly taught conceptual rules and procedures that help students solve problems accurately and efficiently. This matters because learners benefit from faultless communication of cognitive routines that reduce guessing and cognitive load. Clear strategies make success more reliable, practice more productive, and they prevent students from acquiring mis-rules and unnecessary information.
        • To determine the explicitness of a strategy, evaluators should examine where in the program the strategy is first introduced and determine the extent that instruction reliably leads students to the accurate solution for a class of problem types.
        • Explicit Strategy Quality (2-point rubric)
          • ✅ Highly explicit
            • The strategy is introduced before students are required to use it.
            • The strategy is the easiest, most generalizable strategy for the child to perform among alternatives.
            • Steps are unambiguous (students don’t have to infer missing actions), consistently worded, and explicitly modeled.
            • Instruction pre-corrects likely misconceptions and common errors by explicitly stating what not to do, clarifying confusable cases, and highlighting the key features that prevent predictable mistakes.
          • ❌ Not highly explicit
            • Steps are vague, implied, incomplete, or inconsistently worded/modelled.
            • The strategy is unnecessarily cumbersome; it adds unnecessary steps, memory demands, or coordination compared to simpler alternatives.
            • Students must guess what to do next, or rely on intuition and interpretation.
            • The strategy could potentially teach a misconception – e.g., “The last number is the answer”, rather than "The last number is how many are in the set."
      • Does instruction utilize high-milage, generalizable strategies?
        • Generalizable strategies are explicit strategies that apply across a broad, clearly defined family of related problem types and continue to pay off as content becomes more complex. This matters because instructional time and memory are limited: investing in high-mileage explicit strategies reduces the need for students to memorize countless narrow—and often confusable—rules and procedures, without defaulting to overly broad, low-utility heuristics (e.g., “Think about what the problem is asking”).
        • To determine whether a strategy is of appropriate generalizability, evaluators should examine the range of problem types the strategy is intended to solve, and whether the strategy remains effective when surface features change (numbers, contexts, representations, layouts).
        • Explicit Strategy Generalizability (2-point rubric)
          • ✅ Highly generalizable (high mileage)
            • The strategy applies to a family of related problem types (not a single narrow format) and is reused vertically and horizontally across instruction.
            • The strategy holds up when surface features change (numbers, contexts, representations, layouts) and is grounded in underlying structure/relationships.
            • Instructional time spent teaching the strategy clearly pays off by reducing future time spent learning redundant, potentially confusable strategies.
          • ❌ Not highly generalizable (low mileage or too broad)
            • The strategy is tied to a narrow format with limited reuse, or it is so broad it provides little guidance (e.g., “guess and check”, "look at the picture.").
            • Breaks under common variations or does not transfer beyond the taught examples.
            • Time spent does not yield broad payoff in later problem solving.
      • Does instruction utilize effective example selection?
        • Effective example selection means instruction uses well-chosen examples and non-examples to make the critical features of a concept obvious through sameness and difference. This matters because young learners are highly sensitive to surface features; poorly chosen examples can prompt faulty generalizations (mis-rules) and slow acquisition.
        • To determine whether examples are well selected, evaluators should examine whether examples and non-examples are intentionally sequenced to highlight the critical features, whether variation is purposeful (changing only what matters), and whether instruction includes contrasts that prevent predictable confusions.
        • Example Selection (2-point rubric)
          • ✅ Highly effective example selection
            • Examples and non-examples clearly highlight the critical features through systematic contrasts.
            • Example sets begin with clear, representative examples and expand to planned variation and edge-cases only after the rule is established.
            • Variation is purposeful and controlled (irrelevant features are held constant while relevant features change) to anticipate predictable confusions and pre-correct mis-rules.
          • ❌ Not highly effective example selection
            • Examples are one-sided (mostly examples, few/no non-examples) or do not clearly contrast critical features.
            • Example sets are random, with “tricky” items and edge-cases appearing before representative examples are established.
            • Variation is unsystematic, with too many changing features at once or irrelevant “noise” that distracts from the concept.
      • Is practice and review designed to build retention, fluency, and generalization?
        • Practice and review refer to the planned opportunities students have to respond, strengthen recall, build speed/accuracy, and maintain previously learned skills over time. This matters because learning is fragile in PK–3: without sufficient practice and spaced review, students forget, become error-prone, and require reteaching.
        • To determine whether practice and review are well designed, evaluators should examine the amount and distribution of practice, whether review is cumulative and spaced (not just massed), and whether practice targets accuracy first and then fluency, discrimination, and generalization across formats.
        • Practice and Review (2-point rubric)
          • ✅ Highly effective practice and review
            • Students get ample opportunities to respond, with practice progressing from accuracy to fluency to generalization.
            • Review is cumulative and spaced to prevent forgetting (previous skills reappear reliably over time).
            • Practice includes planned variation and mixed formats so students can maintain skills and discriminate between confusable items when surface features change.
          • ❌ Not highly effective practice and review
            • Practice opportunities are too few, overly passive, or do not build toward fluent, reliable performance.
            • Review is minimal, massed, or inconsistent (skills disappear for long stretches and then reappear unexpectedly).
            • Practice stays in one format or one context, with limited discrimination practice, reducing transfer and increasing forgetting outside the practiced form.
    • Curricular Architecture of an App
      • This section of the Early Years PreK3 Brain Lift focuses on the sequencing and design of lessons.
      • It helps to separate two levels of design:
        • Lesson level
          • Lessons should be explicit and DI-aligned: select a granular “atom” of essential content, review prior knowledge, model a high milage next step or concept, check for understanding, provide immediate feedback, provide practice to stabilize performance. This is straightforward.
        • Scope-and-sequence level
          • The scope and sequence is the curricular plan for when content is taught and revisited. There are two common approaches: a conventional massed-practice design (❌) and a track/strand design (✅). You need to understand both to see why track design is more effective.
      • ❌ Here is an example of the conventional approach (as used in Zearn—a curate’s egg of an app). The biggest problem with it, and with many math apps, is the underlying curriculum architecture, which an evaluator can usually spot by scanning the scope and sequence document.
        • In a massed design, topics are covered one at a time. This can look “systematic” because it prioritizes important topics early and tries to follow a logical order.
        • The problem is that students inevitably forget much of what they learn in a massed format. If you dig deeper, you see the same pattern at the lesson level: one main thing per day, maybe some review, maybe some integration of old with new—but not in a planned, retention-driven way.
        • Combined with weak mastery thresholds, students advance with fragile learning and slow long-term progress.
      • ✅ Now compare that scope and sequence with a track design.
        • In track design, topics are categorized by tracks/strands based on the "Big Ideas" of mathematics (counting, addition, facts, etc).
        • Designers make deliberate decisions about how long a topic should be distributed over time, how often it should reappear, and when it should stop being taught in isolation because it has been integrated into later work.
        • "Tracks" as a metaphor work in that the topics cross-cut the entire app, and are not just one-off dots. "Strands" is the other word, and it communicates how the tracks weave together over time, like a rope.
        • Developers are often stuck copying apps that use the traditional (and clearly inferior) massed-practice architecture, like Zearn.
          • Instead, they should build a track design from day one, grounded in learning science: spaced repetition, interleaving, retrieval practice, and incremental introduction based on working-memory constraints.
      • Track Design: Steps for Designing an App
        • Step 1: Define (or locate) enduring tracks that represent the “big ideas” of early math (e.g., counting & quantity, place value, operations, equality, measurement, etc.).
          • Recommended: Use Stein et al., Direct Instruction book to locate the Big Ideas, and the explicit strategies that teach them.
        • Step 2: For each track, atomize the content into the smallest observable objectives (what the child must be able to say, do, select, or type by the end of the lesson), and map prerequisite links between objectives. On any given day, these look like "exercises."
          • Practical deliverable: each track should be represented as a simple table with:
            • Behavioral objectives
            • Prerequisite(s)
            • Lesson type 1 or 2 (1-new learning vs 2-practice/fluency)
            • Mastery check
            • Review schedule
        • Step 3: Author lessons as ~5-minute DI-style routines.
          • The program assumes that nothing is taught in one lesson. Instead, new concepts and skills are presented in two or three consecutive lessons to provide students with enough exposure to new material that they are able to use it in applications.
            • Initial lessons for novel material move from modeling to supported practice to independent practice - I do, we do, you do. (Lesson type 1)
            • Practice lessons focus on fluency and retention. (Lesson type 2)
          • Practical deliverable: each lesson template should include:
            • (1) model (for new material; Lesson type 1)
              • (1a) guided practice
              • (1b) independent practice
            • (1) prompt (for old material; Lesson type 2)
            • (2) mastery check
            • (3) error-correction loop (stop → model correct → retry → recheck)
        • Step 4: Sequence lessons horizontally within each track from simple to complex, based on prerequisites and likely prior knowledge.
          • How many lessons are required to master a topic requires a task analysis of the explicit strategy that is used to solve the problem, and how many countable component atoms are within it.
            • Task analysis: The designer works the skill manually, and counts how many new behaviors and how many new concepts (i.e. definitions, key ideas) are new to the child, based on prerequisites and likely prior knowledge.
              • Rule of thumb: Each new atom requires the generation of a new lesson (see below) that adds the atom to the student's existing repertoire. This is the insight of Direct Instruction and cognitive load theory.
        • Step 5: Assemble daily sessions so multiple short lessons (aka exercises) occur on the same day across tracks. It might look like this:
          • Session Overview (≈27 minutes)
            • Exercise 1 (Track A), 5 minutes — Skill: Count from 10 and end at 32.
            • Exercise 2 (Track B), 5 minutes — Skill: Build and read teen numbers using ten and ones.
            • Exercise 3 (Track C), 7 minutes — Skill: Solve a + b = □ problems by starting on the side without the box, drawing lines, and filling the box.
            • Exercise 4 (Track D), 5 minutes — Skill: Tell time to the hour (o’clock) when the minute hand is on 12.
            • Exercise 5 (Track E: Review Track), Mixed cumulative review - see next Step
        • Step 6: Design a dedicated maintenance/review track that is always cumulative and adaptive - as seen in Exercise 5 above.
          • Practical rule-set (enough to implement):
            • Review selection rule: pull ~60% from “recently taught, not yet stable,” ~30% from “mastered but at risk,” and ~10% from “older mastered.”
            • If error rate exceeds a set threshold, schedule the topic again tomorrow in the review track and downgrade mastery status until performance stabilizes.
        • Step 7: Define mastery assessment
          • When a student passes the mastery check for a given atom, it is to be integrated it into other topics.
            • (e.g., a student responds 90% correct on identifying the equal sign in a symbol ID exercise, and so it becomes a cue inside equation-solving and word problems exercises)
          • When a student passes the mastery check for a given atom, it is then kept alive through the review track.
            • (e.g., identifying the equal sign in a symbol ID exercise still appears in Track E: Review Track for a defined window.
              • NEW: No SRS state — Never practiced
              • LEARNING: Until mastery check is passed — Acquisition
              • REVIEWING: < 30 days — Consolidation
              • RETIRED: ≥ 30 days — Long-term retention
      • Conclusion
        • The result is an mastery-based architecture consisting of a scope-and-sequence of atomized behavioral objectives that appear, reappear, and integrate with purpose—because the app is managing the working memory load of the student, discrimination between different topics, transfer to problems that were not directly instructed, and long-term retention of the material, not just coverage.

    • Spiky POV
      • Concrete → Abstract Is Not a Required Developmental Sequence
        • Introduce abstract structure early. Do not assume young learners must "master" concrete representations before ever being exposed to their abstract counterparts.
        • Abstract examples may be better for promoting transfer, while concrete may be easier to understand initially, boosting immediate performance.
        • Consider concreteness fading (move from concrete → abstract, even in the same lesson), which outperforms staying concrete.
        • Avoid perceptually-rich concrete materials—these often impair transfer and increase distractions.
        • Example sequence of an app that follows this POV
          • 1. Pre-skill instruction on counting dots
            • App: "Touch and count the dots"
          • 2. Abstract instruction to teach deep structure
            • Screen shows: 3 + 2 = ? with plain numerals.
            • App: “We're going to put 3 and 2 together to find the total.”
          • 3. Concrete/pictorial support
            • Under 3 + 2 = ?, the app shows 5 plain dots in a 3-and-2 grouping.
            • App: “Touch and count the dots."
            • Student taps dots, then types answer into the equation.
            • Further trials can vary the surface features of the dots - lines, ants, etc. - for generalization.
          • 4. Return to abstract with embedded concrete/pictorial support.
            • Next problems are only equations: 4 + 3 = ?, 5 + 2 = ?.
            • Instructional hints provide concrete/pictorial support, but by default students work directly with the numbers.
          • 5. Fade concrete supports as mastery criteria is achieved.
            • App: Solve these problems (1 + 3 = ?, 6 + 2 = ?.)

          • Feedback – Tells whether the answer is correct and why.
          • Adaptivity – Adjusts difficulty/support based on current performance.

          • Quizzes / checks for understanding – Embedded questions with feedback to confirm learning.

          • Reflection prompts – Ask the learner to justify or think about their answer.

      • Word Walls
        • Word walls are an extremely instructive element of a reading fluency app. They allow the student to do part-task practice of foundational fluency-building skills—“word work”—apart from the harder task of seeing those same concepts integrated in connected text. Done well, a word wall becomes the app’s “engine room”: the place where accuracy is achieved and later reading becomes less effortful.
        • Not all word walls are created equal. Some are essentially random sets of words (like above), while others are carefully designed to shape what students notice, how they respond, and what they retain.
        • Here are a few guidelines for building word walls that actually do the job.
          • 1) The app utilizes introductory lists and discrimination lists
            • Introductory lists are for teaching something new with minimal interference. Strong programs typically use two introductory formats:
            • New pattern/sound lists: Every word contains the same new spelling–sound pattern so the student can lock onto the target quickly.
              • Example (new digraph sh): ship, shop, shut, wish, cash
              • Example (vowel team ai): rain, sail, train, paint, chain
            • Word-type (structure) introductory lists: Every word shares a word type so students get stable practice with one structure before variation is introduced.
              • Example (CVC): sat, lid, mop, run, web
              • Example (CVCC): mask, tent, lamp, gift, milk
            • Discrimination lists come after introductory lists, and they do a different job: they force the student to read the new pattern amid old patterns. This is where fluency starts to look like real reading—less “spot the trick,” more “choose correctly under pressure.”
              • Example (contrast sh vs ch): ship, chip, shop, chop, wish, rich
              • Example (contrast ai vs short a): rain, ran, sail, sat, train, tank
              • Example (contrast VCV vs CVC): open, hop, even, set, unit, sun
            • A common mistake is building “discrimination” lists where the target pattern is contrasted with something wildly different. Better discrimination mixes items that are minimally different—the exact kind of difference the student must learn to notice.
          • 2) The app utilizes different lists for different instructional goals
            • A word wall should support a goal, and the goal should determine the list design—and the student’s “win condition.”
            • Goal: Decoding/phonics practice (accuracy on a target pattern or structure).
            • Example (final -ck): back, sock, pack, rock, pick
            • Win condition: high accuracy with immediate corrective feedback.
            • Goal: Fluency/automaticity (speed on previously taught patterns).
              • Example (all known patterns): step, plan, grin, swim, crisp
              • Win condition: faster responding on a timed test while keeping accuracy high (no “speed through guessing”).
            • Goal: Mastery check (confirm if student can advance).
              • Example (quick check for ai): rain, paint, chain, sail, train
              • Win condition: meets a preset threshold (e.g., near-perfect accuracy, optionally with a time cap).
            • Goal: Review/maintenance (prevent “perfect forgetting”).
              • Example (older patterns mixed): ship, rain, stop, green, lunch, twist
              • Win condition: stable performance across days/weeks, not just “passed once" so patterns can retire from the program.
          • 3) Not all word walls are vocabulary walls
          • Frankly, a fluency app shouldn't emphasize “vocabulary walls.” Vocabulary lists belong in a vocabulary context (meaning, usage, nuance). In a fluency context, the target is fast, accurate recognition—so a good reading fluency app will rely far more on pattern-driven lists than on meaning-driven lists.
          • Here are the major list families you’ll usually want:
            • Phonics lists (spelling–sound patterns): engineered for efficient decoding practice.
              • Introductory (new pattern ai): rain, sail, train, paint, chain
              • Discrimination (mix ai with known patterns): rain, red, sail, sit, train, top
            • Morphological lists (meaningful word parts): build efficient reading/spelling of longer words by chunks that recur across English.
              • Prefix (re-): redo, replay, reread, rewrite
              • Inflectional suffix (-ed): jumped, helped, packed, brushed
              • Derivational suffix (-tion): action, fraction, extension, motion
            • Sight word lists (high-frequency irregular or not-yet-decodable words): built for instant recognition.
              • Examples: said, does, was, were, one, two
                • Useful contrasts when ready: was/saw; there/three
            • Vocabulary lists (meaning-first words): appropriate when the app’s purpose is explicit word learning.
            • Examples: analyze, compare, reluctant, precise, consequence
              • If your app’s promise is fluency, the word wall should mostly function as a precision tool for decoding, discrimination, and retention. Vocabulary can be supported, but it shouldn’t be the structural centerpiece unless meaning is the product you’re selling.

  • Entry Point Assessment Guidance Brain Lift
    • Purpose
      • Design an entry-point assessment suite that places students into an existing curriculum (e.g., Zearn) so they start where they can succeed and progress efficiently. The key constraint is that we cannot redesign e.g. Zearn’s curricular architecture; we can only (a) decide where to start and (b) decide what to assign next.
    • Core problem
    • Placement is not as simple as “start at Mission X” because many curricula are only partly hierarchical. They contain both:
    • prerequisite chains: where later learning truly depends on earlier learning in a tight sequence, and
    • parallel domains: where topics can be learned somewhat independently (with lighter prerequisites), and where the curriculum’s block sequence is often an editorial choice rather than a cognitive necessity.
    • If we treat a curriculum like a single ladder when it’s actually a mix of ladders + parallel domains, we will misplace students: some will be forced to redo mastered work, while others will be dropped into later work with hidden missing prerequisites.
  • Concrete example: Zearn Grade 3 mission structure is only partly hierarchical
    • One core issue with using “start at Mission X” as the main placement output is that Zearn’s Grade 3 missions are only partly hierarchical.
    • Mission 1 → Mission 3 is clearly hierarchical: “friendly numbers” (easy factors, place-value friendly decompositions, basic strategies) is a sensible prerequisite set for “tricky numbers” (harder facts, less friendly decompositions, more complex regrouping/strategy selection).
      • Some missions are “enabling” rather than strict prerequisites. For example:
        • “Measure It” can support “Find the Area,” because area is a measurement concept and often depends on unit reasoning (and usually some multiplication structure). But you can also teach early area ideas (tiling/counting squares) without having finished a full measurement unit—so it’s not a hard gate.
        • “Find the Area” often depends more on multiplication structure than on measurement per se (especially once you move from counting squares to length × width).
      • Several missions are more like parallel domains than a ladder:
        • “Fractions as Numbers” benefits from strong whole-number sense and partitioning language, but it’s not inherently downstream of “area” or “data.”
        • “Display Data” is largely its own domain (reading graphs, interpreting scales, representing data) with lighter prerequisites.
        • “Shapes and Measurement” overlaps with “Measure It” and can be revisited throughout, rather than “completed” once.
    • Implication: a single mission-level start point is a lossy output
      • A “start at Mission X” placement output collapses a multi-dimensional readiness profile into one number. That tends to fail in two common ways:
      • False remediation (too low)
        • A student who is strong in multiplication/division but weaker in fractions may get pushed far back because the mission order bundles and blocks topics. Result: wasted time, lower motivation, “I already know this” repetition, slower time-to-mastery.
      • Hidden prerequisite gaps (too high)
        • A student may be placed into a later mission because they appear competent in one domain, but they are missing a specific enabling skill (e.g., multiplication structure needed for area). Result: brittle performance, high error rates, and churn.
    • A good placement system therefore does two jobs:
      • 1. Diagnose readiness on key prerequisite chains (hard gates).
      • 2. Diagnose coverage across parallel domains (soft gates), so we can assign targeted catch-up without forcing the student backward globally.
  • Recommended architecture for an entry-point assessment suite
    • Build a prerequisite map behind the scenes
    • Even if the product only exposes Missions, the assessment should internally tag items such as:
      • multiplication/division structure (equal groups, arrays, unknown factor, etc.)
      • place value / decomposition / regrouping
      • fraction concepts (unit fractions, number line, equivalence foundations)
      • measurement & unit reasoning
      • area concepts (tiling → formula structure)
      • data/graphs
      • geometry vocabulary/attributes
    • This map is what allows the assessment to detect “you’re fine here, but you’re missing this one enabling piece.”
    • Two-layer assessment: gates + domain probes
      • Layer A: prerequisite chain gates (fast, high signal)
        • Use short checkpoint screens that determine whether the student can safely operate in the next stretch of the chain (e.g., friendly → tricky computation; multiplication structure before area-by-formula).
      • Layer B: parallel domain probes (broad but shallow)
        • Run brief probes across parallel domains to detect obvious gaps that would later cause failure, without forcing a global reset.
        • The output should be a placement profile, not a single start point.
        • Translate the placement profile into Zearn assignments
  • Because we can’t change Zearn, the practical move is to output:
    • Primary start point: the earliest mission/lesson zone that is safe for the main prerequisite chain the student is on.
    • Targeted “patch” assignments: specific mission lesson sets (or teacher-led supplements) that address gaps in parallel domains or enabling skills.
    • A review plan: a small amount of cumulative mixed review to prevent “pass-then-forget” (especially when the student skips large segments).
  • Bottom line
    • Why the “start at Mission X” plan tends to suck (and what to do instead)
    • What goes wrong in the simplistic plan
    • It assumes the mission order equals the learning order.
    • It treats parallel domains like prerequisites.
    • It can’t express “start later for computation but earlier for fractions,” so it either over-remediates or over-accelerates.
    • In partially hierarchical curricula like Zearn, the right assessment design is not “find the right mission.” It’s “find the student’s readiness across prerequisite chains and parallel domains, then translate that into a safe start point plus targeted patching.” That’s how you reduce wasted time and improve time-to-mastery without pretending the curriculum is a single ladder.
