      - (11/16 pages) Research Study:
        - The Media Richness Theory: Media Richness Theory posits that communication media vary in their ability to convey information based on factors such as immediacy of feedback, multiple cues, and personalization.
        - Because of Pinterest's rich visual content and highly interactive and responsive features, it's highly effective in "Media Richness"
          - Insight: THIS is a concrete example as to why Pinterest is actually being used by businesses. Not only that, but specifically businesses marketing to Gen Z
        - Gen Z reacts and responds to high concentrations of knowledge and creative energy all at once. Because Pinterest is high in "Media Richness" it directly suggests exactly why Gen Z appreciates it.
        - Gen Z isn't getting slow doses of inspiration on pinterest, they're getting rich, compact bursts of lots of information at once
          - This is a strategy used by older marketers - and it clearly is just adapted here - that works like this: Marketers constantly bombard customers with new information: i.e. placing lots of smaller products in the checkout line so you're more inclined to buy them (and feel pressure from other shopper in line to buy them, too).
        - In the context of creative tasks (and in turn, buying decisions), Pinterest's comprehensive, fast-paced UI and framework stimulate ideas of eitehr creativity or, literally, "I want to buy that!"
          - Media Richness Theory highlights that the effectiveness of a medium also depends on how well the medium fits the tasks at hand
            - Pinterest uses AI to always make sure that it is focusing its users on the task at hand. Or, otherwise, it guides users to where it thinks they need to be to put buyers and creators in the right frame of mind to find what they need or make a purchase
          - Pinterest is all about control - controlling effectiveness and media richness.
        - Developing a design project or creating an art piece requires a medium that can convey nuanced and detailed information.
          - This is exactly what makes Pinterest effective.
        - Pinterest's search engine is designed to surface visually rich content that aligns with users' interests and queries.
          - Much like TikTok, which is effective because IT is fast-paced and HIGHLY searched optimized, Pinterest uses this same skill. The difference? The audience (sometimes).
          - What does it mean by this: The ability to discover new content and ideas easily helps to keep creative juices flowing. (i.e. stay on the app!)
        - Pinterest effectively trains creatives. What does this mean? As users surf their highly-curated feed, they must make decisions about whether pins are "good enough", "aesthetic enough" etc. Users don't blindly pin - or at least they don't in the beginning. The longer that users (regardless of how versed they are on any other social media platform) stay on Pinterest, using the app daily, the better they get at subconsciously curating.
          - Insight: Subconscious curators: Every person on the app, whether or not YOU are above them in hierarchy (I.e. YOU are trying to sell to THEM) become curators of content as they use the app. As users learn to refine their feed (just like a TikTok algorithm) they also begin to refine their choice whether or not to pin things. What once inspired them months ago on their Pinterest may not be as visually stimulating anymore, or, not good enough to save, not because of a stylistic change but because of the small impacts that Pinterest has on the mind every day using the app. One becomes more perceptive of a bad-quality image, of misplaced text, etc.
          - In this sense, everyone on the app is a curator. And me, as a seller, needs to keep in mind that older followers are more alert towards what IS and IS NOT aesthetic or "of the vibe"
- What is Parke and what can we learn from them?
  - Parke, Case Study
    - Parke started as a re-work jeans brand that quickly turned into an internet sensation going viral all over TikTok because of their crewneck cuts. Parke utilizes founder-led marketing, as their founder, Chelsea PARKE, is an influencer. After starting a few years ago, Parke truly blew up this year due to their sheer volume of drops and how popular they've gotten over the past 11 months.
      - With partnerships ranging from Breast Cancer Research to Set Active, Parke takes advantage of just how much their audience shops... Because THEY DO.
        - Parke knows their audience, and through their TikTok it's pretty obvious. Wealthy college sorority girls who are already shopaholics. Or; 18-25, wealthy bracket, shop a lot (lots of clothes), are on TikTok, in a sorority, love a city lifestyle, shop with athleisure brands already
      - How I found Parke: When thinking about my own biases and consumer psychology, it becomes pretty easy to discern consumer focus: I'm very interested in college lifestyle, and therefore, Parke outfit videos continue to frequent my FYP. I'm not yet in college, but because I want to feel part of that certain group, I was influenced to get Parke.
        - This is a phenomenon I call aspirational branding - and it has an effect that extends age range for target market DOWNwards. I am younger than Parke's target market. So, in order to feel included, I too buy Parke sweatshirts. Aside from the fact that I myself am conscious of how frequently I buy brands that are purposefully aspirational, *this* is the reason why Parke is blowing up. They are aspirational, and it's a foolproof way from brands to incite FOMO and "groupness" - that is, making customers feel like they're part of an exclusive club without necessarily using limited drops. So, Parke has two age ranges, an intended age range (18-25) and a gross age range (15-27).
          - Because of this, you feel like you're part of a certain group when you buy, will be more inclined to buy future products because of this, and will want to post about your experiences. 
          - FOMO: Parke leverages a sister concept to aspirationalism called FOMO which really includes very limited edition clothing drops. Parke leverages holidays, events, special months, and other abstract grounders to make their drops feel more cemented into the minds of consumers. Shopaholics are more likely to purchase something if they can find a way to justify it. Holidays, special dates, months, etc. can do this. That's why Black Friday and Christmas are a brand's biggest season. Parke does this all year, and it makes customers care enough to buy. Football season means Parke sweatshirts with colors. Valentines day means a drop. Even Halloween means another limited drop.
            - In this sense, they function like any streetwear brand. But, Parke kind of disguises these tactics by how limited and simple each drop is.
      - Algorithm favoring: Parke is just controversial and small enough in a bigger category that their audience is able to extend inside of a bigger context. TikTok picks up on this and makes sure than heavily engaged Parke videos are pushed alongside other Parke videos. I.e: When one college girl makes a video with a Parke mockneck, and you engage, you'll see more college girls in Parke mocknecks. Parke is easily identifiable to a fickle algorithm.
        - UGC: Parke has talking points. When girls buy Parke, they WANT to post about it. It's JUST cool enough to post about. Because of this, there's a high volume of content around Parke on TikTok.
      - Parke engages + reposts: Parke is a mastermind when it comes to customer service and reposting user content. They do it efficiently and frequently, enough so that customers and PCs can pick up on it

- Oren John and building a brand
  - Luxury Brand Video - 7 pillars of a luxury brand
  - Pillar 1 - luxury is about quality and not about price "bespoke"
  - Pillar 2 - design - needs to have aspiration and execution on a design that is really special - that has a story. goes from designer brand to luxury brand - this is important. T have an amazing design you really do need a team
  - Needs to be desirable - this ties into be pillar 4 of resale value, what is the resale value of the piece? Is it an INVESTMENT! This is really an extra sauce - control this experience of resale.
  - This is applicable! You can control this resale by controlling it yourself. Djerf Avenue has a resale place - this automatically makes people associate it was environmentally friendly and YES luxury. 
  - Time - Luxury is what you build to last generations, like the products. It isn't really a thing you build to SELL
  - 2 notable brands - Jacquemus and Flamingo Estate
    - These brands are actually really interesting - notably Flamingo Estate (i've heard of Jacquemus)
    - FE is like the Aesop of LA - they sold a high-end subscription, farm to table veggies for the LA elite. Using this platform they were able to relase CPG and get into retail with high quality veggie wash, etc.
    - This is just a really great example of mixing design and desirability, as well as product market fit without needing the time aspect.
  - Insight: Architecture is a really interesting way to include Lore. it's like this for Flamingo Estate - which is based off of a really magnetic place - the Flamingo Estate. You can also think about Pop Up shop designs and what those are set up like. You can even think about interior of store.
  - Experience - a luxury store having you talk to a rep, the Aesop and Glossier in-store experience which differentiates it. 
  - "Connotation of a brand" - not just what a customer feels, but what are the thoughts, the moodboard that comes to mind when you think of a brand. Is it messy? Make it simpler and more recognizable then. Make it YOU.
