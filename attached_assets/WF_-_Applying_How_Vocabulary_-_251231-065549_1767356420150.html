Applying How Vocabulary is Learned to our Vocabulary Program
  • Owner
    • Marcello Sgambelluri
  • Purpose
    • This Brainlift will outline a strategy to apply the key principles from How Vocabulary is Learned and related research to our current and future vocabulary program.
    • Replace the inefficient, "grade-level" model of vocabulary instruction with a high-velocity, frequency-based system designed for the 2 Hour Learning (2HL) environment.
    • Shift the definition of mastery from passive recognition to "Productive Retrieval," ensuring students can spell, use, and apply academic vocabulary (Academic Word List AWL/Test Word List TWL) with precision.
    • Integrate vocabulary as the "operating system" of the curriculum, using explicit drills to front-load the specific words students need to unlock comprehension in Science, Math, History, etc.
    • Show how frequency, adaptive learning burden, context driven integration, productive retrieval, and Nation's four strands can improve our programming. See "DOK 3 - Insights" for application of these.
    • In-Scope (What we are solving for)
      • Target Audience: Native English Speakers (L1) and fluent English speakers in Grades 3–12. Not L2s.
      • Target Vocabulary: High-utility Academic Vocabulary (AWL, Tier 2) 3K+ words, and Subject-Specific terms (Tier 3) required for Science, History, and Literature.
      • Mechanism of Action: Deliberate Practice (drills), Productive Retrieval (typing/spelling), and Spaced Repetition.
      • Integration: Strategies for pre-teaching vocabulary to ensure 98% text coverage before reading content modules.
    • Out-of-Scope (What we are NOT doing)
      • Foundational Literacy: This is not a "Learning to Read" (phonics/decoding) intervention for early readers; it assumes basic reading fluency.
      • Survival Vocabulary: We are not teaching the 1,000–2,000 most frequent words (e.g., the, house, run) generally needed for ESL/ELL beginners.
      • Passive Assessment: We do not cover multiple-choice recognition strategies or standard "vocab quizzes."
      • General Reading Strategies: We are not addressing "reading for pleasure" or broad literacy skills outside of the specific vocabulary acquisition mechanics needed for 2HL efficiency.
  • DOK4 - Spiky POVs
    • The "Fluency Trap": Student oral fluency is masking a massive Orthographic Gap that invalidates standard reading instruction.
      • For native speakers a focus on precise use and spelling is essential (over deep concept mapping)
        • Reading scores will flatline until we stop teaching students "comprehension strategies" and start teaching them rapid orthographic mapping (connecting their complex oral library to written forms).
        • (Kate Nation: Lexical Quality). Low-quality lexical representations (vague spelling) cause processing drag that mimics comprehension failure.
      • Teachers assume native speakers "know" words like 'establish' or 'previous'. They don't. They know the sound, but they possess low Lexical Quality (they can't spell it, can't use it precisely, and glaze over it in text).
      • Teachers also assume they will learn these through incidental interactions and reading. But they need deliberate instruction
      • Stop treating native speakers like "advanced readers" and start treating them like Code Breakers. The goal isn't "concept acquisition" (they know what previous means); the goal is Orthographic Mapping (locking the spelling to the meaning). This shifts instruction from "Teaching Definitions" (slow) to "Precision Drills" (fast).
    • The 'Skill' Fallacy: Schools treat Vocabulary like a 'Muscle' to be exercised, but the brain treats it like a 'Database' to be indexed. This Category Error guarantees inefficiency.
      •  Modern education is obsessed with "skills" (critical thinking, creativity) and has wrongly lumped vocabulary into that bucket. They treat words like basketball free-throws—something you "practice" and "get the gist of" over time. This is disastrous. You cannot "practice" a password; you either have the key or you don't. Treating a binary data object (a word) like a fuzzy skill is why students spend 12 years "practicing" English and still read at a 3rd grade level.
      • We must reclassify Vocabulary from "Pedagogy" to "Information Theory." A word isn't a "skill" to be nurtured; it is a Cryptographic Key required to decrypt a text. If the key is corrupt (bad spelling) or missing (unknown concept), the decryption fails.
      • We stop "teaching" vocabulary and start "installing" it. Our goal isn't "rich engagement"; it is Lossless Data Transfer. We use Engelmann's algorithms to verify that the "file" (the word) was written correctly to the student's long-term memory, bit-for-bit (spelling-for-meaning). With this, we "upload" this data at 2x velocity. We stop "teaching" and start "installing" the lexicon required for academic thriving.
    • "Grade-Level Vocabulary" is an Administrative Fiction to convenience adults and teachers, an obsolete relic of print-based logistics
      • Grade levels exist only because physical textbooks couldn't adapt. In an AI world, "Grade 4 Vocabulary" is a zombie category. There is only "Frequency Horizon" (what you need next) and "Domain Utility" (what you need for this science unit).
        • The "Remedial Class" will disappear. It will be replaced by "Frequency Band Filling." A 9th grader reading at a 4th-grade level doesn't need "baby books"; they need a rapid injection of the 2K-5K Frequency Bands to unlock age-appropriate content. We treat vocabulary gaps like software dependencies, not developmental delays.
        • (Paul Nation: Frequency). The statistical reality of language ignores grade levels; 85% of text is the 1K list, regardless of the book's "age rating."
      • We must ignore grade level lists for any student who has not mastered AWL, TWL, or the 3k/4k+ list.
      • System rule: no student advances to 4th grade vocabulary without mastering the 3k/4k (5k/6k possibly), AWL, and TWL (Test Word List) lists
        • We cannot explicitly teach these up front, they must be assessed managably because of size
          • 2,000 words (3k/4k), 571 words (AWL), TBD words (TWL)
          • Emphasis should be put on the 571 AWL and xxx TWL words and mastery should be able to be shown first (then mastered words are only spiraled never explicitly taught)
          • 3k/4k lists can be quizzed over time as drills and mastered words are only spiraled
      • We should teach words solely based on frequency. This is very true for non-native speakers the first 1,000 most frequent words cover ~85% of text; the second 1,000 cover ~4%. Our students should have natural fluency in the 1k and 2k list (as native speakers of english). We should focus on the Academic Word List(AWL) and 3k and 4k list.
        • If there is an inline seamless way to test for the 1k/2k list through alphawrite, alphavocab, alpharead, etc. if any 1k/2k words are identified as unknown those should become top priority for vocab learning and supplant any other current word list.
        • Testing for the entire 1k/2k list explicitly would be cumbersome and likely a waste of time for native speakers.
          • We must be religious in implementing pareto's principle in our vocabulary instruction (85% of the benefit coming from 1,000 words)
          • Teaching low frequency academic words (e.g. photosynthesis) to a student who hasn't mastered high frequency verbs (e.g. establish, maintain) creates lexical gaps that stall comprehension.
          • Words should be taught in dissociated groups ("food" or "animals" should not be taught together, the more similar the further apart they should be, i.e. apricot, nectarine, and apple should not be introduced near each other as new vocabulary)
    • Engagement is the enemy of Velocity: The EdTech "Engagement Model" is a conflict of interest, product efficacy must be measured by 'Time-to-Exit,' not 'Time-on-Site'
      • Multiple-Choice Assessment creates a 'Data Mirage' that destroys AI adaptability
        • Creating Data integrity is key to AI instruction:
          • Adaptive algorithms (AI) rely on clean signal data. A correct guess on a multiple-choice question introduces "False Positive Noise" into the algorithm, causing it to skip necessary repetitions. This corruption compounds over time, leading to the "Illusion of Competence" collapse in later grades seen in traditional education models
          • Productive Retrieval (typing the word) is not just a pedagogical preference; it is a Data Integrity Requirement. We cannot build a valid adaptive engine on the shaky foundation of recognition testing. We treat assessment as a binary cryptographic key: You have the key (the word), or you don't.
        • Replace the "Select" button with the "Type" bar. Productive Retrieval is the only valid metric in a high-performance system. Mastery is binary: Can you produce the word from thin air (typing or speaking) without a word bank? If not, you don't know it. We stop grading the ability to guess and start grading the ability to generate.
          • retrieval should be the primary mode of vocabulary learning
            • Seeing a word is weak learning (input)
          • Productive Retrieval is best (seeing meaning --> forcing output of word)
            • Receptive retrieval is good (see the word --> think of meaning)
            • Passive reading or multiple choice are often wastes of time
        • For Native English Speakers, multiple choice tests are actively deceptive. It essentially tests "process of elimination" skills, not vocabulary. A student can score 100% on a definitions quiz and still be totally unable to use 'unprecedented' in an essay or spell 'bureaucracy'.
        • A word is not mastered until a student has typed or spoken the word in a productive retrieval task (no word bank, this likely needs to use spiraling)
      • Not every word deserves a deep, multi-modal teaching strategy. We need adaptively and intelligently modulate the "Learning burden" of each word.
        • Efficiency comes from matching activities to learning burden.
      • Current vocabulary apps (Membean, NoRedInk) optimize for Retention (DAU/MAU) using gamification. This creates a perverse incentive to teach slowly. A true "2HL" tool optimizes for Exit Velocity, getting the student out of the app and into the text as fast as possible.
      • We are not a "learning game." We are a "Knowledge Injector." Our metric of success is how quickly we render our own tool unnecessary for that specific text. Boredom is efficient; dopamine is a delay tactic.
      • Sort words into those that require Quick and Easy activities and those that require Deep Processing activities
        • Don't spend 10 minutes teaching effect vs affect, drill the difference fast
    • Myths
      • You can "read your way" to vocabulary mastery
        • The incidental learning hypothesis isn't fast enough for 2HL and is inefficient for rapid and high quality vocabulary acquisition. Data from How Vocabulary is Learned (HVL) shows that without massive volume (on the order of 1 million words/year) we must rely on high quality and targeted vocab instruction. Even if our students get that 1 million words, to 2x learning we must deliberately practice to outpace "natural" (traditional) learning rates.
          • Deliberate learning of vocab in isolation and integration creates significantly quicker vocabulary acquisition and in turn reading comprehension and general knowledge acquisition.
          • We can not wait for the 12+ repetitions for incidental learning to happen naturally.
          • We must create a high quality and explicit teaching engine to be the acquisition mechanism of vocabulary and incidental learning as the maintenance mechanism.
          • We must value words then put them into a high quality, research backed, instructional model.
      • "We should teach vocabulary in logical groups, like 'Food Words' or 'Clothing Items', to help students make connections."
        • Teaching semantic sets (related words) together actually hinders learning due to Interference Theory. HVL cites research showing that learning similar words together (e.g., apricot, peach, nectarine) makes it 50–100% harder to retain them because the brain struggles to distinguish between them.
        • In a 2HL model, words should be taught based on frequency and unconnected contexts to maximize retention speed.
      • "A student 'knows' a word if they can pass a multiple-choice definition test."
        • "Mastery" must be defined as productive retrieval (generating the word from scratch), which requires a higher burden of proof.
        • Vocabulary should be assessed, where possible, in open formats and writing (either in the learning app vs. Mastery Track or in AlphaWrite). This creates a tension of mastery being student open ended answers vs. standardized test responses being multiple choice. Need a balance with a focus on open retrieval.
        • This is the "illusion of competence" where a student thinks they know a word but can't define it independently, spell it correctly, or use it correctly.
  • DOK3 - Insights
    • Intuition fails education placing words together into beautifully packaged themed units that fail students
      • There is a fundamental conflict between Teacher Intuition (grouping words by taxonomy, e.g., "Food Unit") and Cognitive Architecture (Interference Theory) along with AI deliberate vocab instruction. Teachers group words to "help connections," but research shows this can increase learning time by 50–100% because the brain struggles to distinguish similar concepts learned simultaneously. If we are trying to install an individual word we should minimize taxonomic grouping if we are deepening understanding taxonomy can have a boost to outcomes (especially in younger grades and with tier 1 words)
      • Our competitive advantage lies in doing the counter-intuitive thing: While competitors build "Themed Units" (which sell well but teach poorly) we use an AI enforced frequency-based, dissociated batching system implemented through Engelmen's algorithm. We prioritize velocity of retention over the aesthetics of the curriculum.
      • Our word lists should be based on Frequency (1k/2k/3k/4k/etc. lists) and context (Academic Word List - AWL)
        • A standardized test frequency list would be ideal, have talked to Joshua Mitchell about this using common crawl
          • What are the most frequent words in G3-G12 tests, SAT, and NWEA? Use those as foundational vocab lists.
        • Possible non-negotiable: No student goes to 4th grade vocab without mastering 1k-4k+ (5k/6k could be the cutoff), AWL, and Test Word List (TWL).
    • Words should have adaptive activities based on learning burden of the word
      • Traditional tools treat every unknown word as a "blank slate," forcing students through the same long instructional loop for every word. This is inefficient for Native Speakers (L1), who usually have the concept (oral mastery) but lack the precision (Lexical Quality/Spelling). Teaching the concept of "bureaucracy" to a 10th grader is waste; teaching the spelling is value.
      • Strategic Implication: The AI must dynamically adjust the Learning Burden.
        • High Burden (New Concept, Difficult Word): Use Engelmann’s "Minimal Pairs" to teach meaning (e.g., oligarchy).
        • Low Burden (Vocabulary Gap): Use rapid-fire Orthographic Drills to map spelling/usage (e.g., persist).
      • This variable pacing allows us to hit 2x velocity where competitors are stuck explaining concepts students already know.
    • Our assessments and apps need to prioritize productive retrieval
      • Most platforms use multiple-choice for efficiency, but Interference Theory suggests that presenting "distractors" (wrong answers that look like the right answer) actively corrupts memory. It forces the brain to encode the wrong association to reject it, creating "Interference Drag." Furthermore, passive recognition allows L1 students to "fake" mastery using context clues without truly knowing the word. We should use this solely as test prep and not knowledge generation.
      • Strategic Implication: We reject multiple-choice not just because it allows guessing, but because it is unsafe. Our assessment model relies strictly on Productive Retrieval (typing/speaking the word from a prompt). This is the only "clean signal" that proves the student has mapped the specific orthography (spelling) without the noise of distractors.
      • Moving from just recognition to generation
      • producing a meaning from a word is more powerful than producing a word from it's definition
      • actively use words in context (write a sentence or paragraph with vocab words from scratch, ideally without seeing the words)
    • Words need to be contextualized fast, connecting alphawrite, alpharead, science, math, etc. content to synergize with alphavocab.
      • Strategic Synthesis: Vocabulary is the "Operating System" of the Curriculum, Not a Siloed App.
        • Synthesis: Research confirms that reading comprehension fails where background knowledge and vocabulary fail. Treating vocabulary as an isolated "subject" creates a transfer gap. Words learned in isolation decay rapidly if they are not immediately "instantiated" in a meaningful context.
        • Strategic Implication: We treat vocabulary as the Pre-Requisite for Content. The AI acts as a "Gate," scanning every Science/History text and triggering a Pre-Teaching Intervention for any gap words. We do not allow students to "struggle through" a text with <98% coverage; that is cognitively inefficient "waste" in a 2HL model. Words learned in the Drill App must immediately unlock a specific module in Science, Reading, History etc. (or vice versa). If a student is working on trigonometry in math Alphavocab is also working on vocab words from trig.
    • Our vocab program needs to intelligently implement Nation's four strands in novel counterintutive ways
      • Synthesis: Paul Nation’s "Four Strands" typically suggests a balanced 25% split. However, in a Time-Compressed (2HL) environment, we cannot wait for the slow accumulation of "Meaning-Focused Input" (Reading) to build the initial lexicon.
      • Strategic Implication: We intentionally overweight the "Language-Focused Learning" (Drills) strand at the beginning of a module. We use deliberate practice as an Injection Mechanism to front-load the vocabulary required for the unit. This allows the subsequent Reading/Writing strands (Input/Output) to function at full speed, unhindered by decoding pauses. We don't just "balance" the strands; we sequence them for acceleration.
        • Language-Focused Learning: AI drills new words (tyranny, militia) --> Velocity
        • Meaning-Focused Input: Student reads a history text using those words --> Context
        • Meaning-Focused Output: Student writes an essay using those words --> Mastery
        • Fluency Development: Student works with easy/familiar material to increase speed and smoothness of processing --> Retention.

    • Category 1: The Vocab Acceleration Strategy: Frequency Over Grade Level
      • Summary:
        • This category defines what words to teach. It replaces "grade-level" lists with frequency bands and utility.
      • Source 1: Paul Nation - Structure & Frequency (BNC/COCA Lists)
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • The first 1,000 word families cover ~85% of conversational English and ~70% of written text.
          • The second 1,000 (2K) adds only ~4-5% coverage.
          • Native speakers typically enter school knowing 3,000–5,000 word families orally.
          • Vocabulary beyond the 2K band drops off rapidly in frequency but increases in specific semantic value.
        • DOK 2 – summary
          • Because L1 learners already possess the 1K/2K bands orally, teaching these lists (e.g., the, house, run) is "waste" in the 2HL model.
          • Supports SPOV: We must ignore grade level lists for any student who has not mastered the 3K/4K/AWL lists first.
          • Supports SPOV: We should teach words solely based on frequency.
        • link: https://www.eapfoundation.com/vocab/general/bnccoca/
      • Source 2: Averil Coxhead - The Academic Word List (AWL)
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • The AWL contains 570 word families that appear with high frequency across all academic disciplines (Arts, Science, Law, etc.).
          • These words account for ~10% of the total words in academic texts.
          • They are "Tier 2" words: high utility but low frequency in casual conversation.
        • DOK 2 – summary
          • The AWL is the "high-leverage" target for Grade 3+ native speakers. Mastery of these 570 families creates a multiplier effect on comprehension across all subjects.
          • Supports SPOV: "Context is for using words, not guessing them." (Knowing AWL reduces the need to guess).
        • link: https://www.wgtn.ac.nz/lals/resources/academicwordlist
    • Category 2: The Efficiency Engine - Conditions of Acquisition
      • Summary:
        • This category defines how to teach for speed. It contrasts incidental learning (too slow) with deliberate learning (velocity) and establishes the rules for grouping words.
      • Source 1: Stuart Webb - Incidental vs. Deliberate Learning
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • Incidental learning (learning just by reading) requires 12+ encounters to learn a single word.
          • L1 learners typically need to read ~1 million words per year to maintain natural growth rates.
          • Deliberate learning (flashcards, drills) yields significantly larger gains in shorter timeframes compared to reading alone.
        • DOK 2 – summary
          • Relying on "reading time" for vocabulary acquisition is mathematically impossible within a 2-hour academic day. Deliberate practice must be the primary acquisition engine.
          • Supports Myth: "Reading your way to vocabulary mastery is a myth."
          • Supports Truth: "Rich Instruction is often a waste of time" (Deliberate drills are faster).
        • link: https://www.eapfoundation.com/vocab/learn/incidental/
      • Source 2: Higa & Nation - Interference Theory
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • Teaching words in semantic sets (e.g., colors, fruits, opposites) increases learning difficulty by 50–100%.
          • The brain struggles to distinguish between similar items learned simultaneously (cross-association).
          • Interference persists over time if the initial encoding was clustered.
          • This is contested in research, it is certain that grouping hurts retention in adults learning english as a second language but can help children building conceptual knowledge or in deep study (not just learning a new vocabulary word but learning it in context and in usage). When growing vocabulary depth:
            • Taxonomy grouping is most effective: organized by type (vegetables)
            • Theme grouping is less effective: organized by association or event (growing things or flying things)
            • Children can use shared features to get a head start on new words
        • DOK 2 – summary
          • The AI must actively scramble vocabulary lists. Grouping by "theme" (Food Unit) is an anti-pattern that slows retention.
          • Contradicts Myth: "We should teach vocabulary in logical groups to help students make connections."
        • link:https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0346251X08000420
    • Category 3: Task Architecture - Mastery & Retrieval
      • Summary:
        • This category defines mastery. It moves the standard from passive recognition (multiple choice) to productive retrieval (generation), ensuring L1 speakers cannot "fake" knowledge.
      • Source 1: Griffin & Harley / Webb - Retrieval Modes
        • DOK 1 - facts
          • Receptive Retrieval (seeing word --> recalling meaning) is easier and decays faster.
          • Productive Retrieval (seeing meaning --> producing word) is cognitively harder but leads to stronger retention.
          • The "Testing Effect" shows that the act of retrieval itself strengthens the neural pathway.
        • DOK 2 - summary
          • Passive multiple-choice tests allow for guessing and do not verify if a student can use the word. 2HL requires Productive Retrieval (typing/saying) to verify the "Lexical Quality" (spelling/precision of use).
          • Supports Truth: "If you aren't struggling, you aren't learning."
          • Contradicts Myth: "A student 'knows' a word if they can pass a multiple-choice definition test."
        • link: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/303939278_The_Effects_of_Receptive_and_Productive_Word_Retrieval_Practice_on_Second_Language_Vocabulary_Learning
      • Source 2: Kate Nation – The Lexical Quality Hypothesis
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • High-quality lexical representations include precise orthography (spelling), phonology (sound), and semantics (meaning).
          • Low-quality representations (knowing the "gist" but not the spelling) slow down reading processing speeds.
          • L1 speakers often suffer from high semantic knowledge but low orthographic precision.
        • DOK 2 – summary
          • For native speakers, the "gap" is often spelling and precision, not basic meaning. Drills must focus on these "quality" aspects to improve reading fluency.
          • Supports Truth: "Rich Instruction is often a waste of time" (Focus on precision/spelling for L1s, not deep concept mapping).

    • Category 4: Comprehension Gating – The 98% Threshold
      • Summary:
        • This category defines the rules of engagement for reading. It establishes the boundary between "learning to read" and "reading to learn."
      • Source 1: Hu & Nation – Coverage Thresholds
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • 98% coverage (1 unknown word in 50) is the threshold for unassisted comprehension of written text.
          • 95% coverage is adequate for spoken discourse but insufficient for academic reading.
          • At 90% coverage (1 in 10 unknown), comprehension drops significantly, and guessing from context becomes statistically random.
        • DOK 2 – summary
          • "Guessing from context" is an ineffective strategy for acquisition below the 98% threshold. The AI must pre-teach gap words to ensure the student is actually reading, not decoding.
          • If a student reads a text with 90% coverage they aren't learning they are decoding gibberish.
            • Context is for using words not guessing them
            • You must know 95-98% of surrounding words to successfully guess the meaning of an unknown word.
            • AI must analyze text before a student sees it, ensuring 98% coverage, preteaching any specific vocabulary gaps
            • For native speakers this preteaching is simply mapping spelling to oral meaning (rather than teaching concept from scratch) as it is usually only a Lexical Gap (they know the word verbally but not in writing)
          • Supports Truth: "Context is for using words, not guessing them."
        • link: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11145-022-10385-0
    • Category 5: Curriculum Integration – Context & Application
      • Summary:
        • This category defines the lifecycle of a word in the system. It connects the isolated drill (Language-Focused Learning) to the broader curriculum (Meaning-Focused Input/Output).
      • Source 1: E.D. Hirsch – Knowledge & Context
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • Reading comprehension is highly correlated with background knowledge and domain-specific vocabulary.
          • "General" reading strategies do not compensate for a lack of vocabulary.
        • DOK 2 – summary
          • Vocabulary cannot be an isolated "subject." Words learned in the efficiency drills must be immediately "instantiated" in Science/History modules to lock in the conceptual knowledge.
        • link: https://www.aft.org/ae/spring2006/hirsch
      • Source 2: Paul Nation – The Four Strands
        • DOK 1 – facts
          • A balanced course requires: Meaning-Focused Input (Reading/Listening), Meaning-Focused Output (Writing/Speaking), Language-Focused Learning (Drills), and Fluency Development.
          • "Language-Focused Learning" (drills) should only be ~25% of the time but is the most efficient for initial acquisition.
          • Meaning-Focused Input (Reading & Listening): The student consumes high-knowledge content (Science, History) to build conceptual understanding, with the AI ensuring 98% vocabulary coverage beforehand to prevent frustration.
          • Meaning-Focused Output (Writing & Speaking): The student is required to use recently learned academic vocabulary in original essays and discussions to force the brain to retrieve and lock in precise meaning.
          • Language-Focused Learning (Deliberate Drills): The student engages in rapid-fire, high-intensity drills focused on spelling, usage, and definitions to accelerate the acquisition of new academic words faster than reading alone permits.
          • Fluency Development (Speed & Flow): The student practices speed-reading or rapid-response drills with already known material to make word recognition automatic, freeing up mental energy for complex problem-solving.
        • DOK 2 – summary
